About Me

I'm Luca Recchia, a Bioengineering student at Northeastern University with a focus on design, prototyping, and hands-on problem solving. I'm drawn to the full lifecycle of a product — from the first sketch to a working prototype — and I've spent the last few years building that skill set through increasingly complex engineering challenges.

My design experience started with coursework in SolidWorks and AutoCAD, where I learned to translate ideas into precise 3D models ready for fabrication. That foundation quickly grew into real-world application: I've designed and 3D-printed custom mechanical components, built Arduino-powered electromechanical systems, and engineered structural solutions under real field constraints. With Engineering Without Borders, I designed a water tank stand for a community in Uganda, working within the limits of steep terrain and limited resources to create something that could actually be built and maintained on-site. During my time studying abroad in London, I took two design projects from concept to completion — an accessible automated waste disposal system and a maze-navigating robot — each requiring rapid iteration, prototyping, and integration of electronics with physical assemblies.

I'm always looking for the next thing to design and build, whether that's a mechanical system, a piece of infrastructure, or a song — I also sing with Distilled Harmony and produce music in my free time.

Water Tank Stand — Engineering Without Borders

EWB Uganda Project · Fall 2025

As a designer on the Engineering Without Borders team, I helped develop a structurally sound water tank stand to support and stabilize potable water storage for a rural community in Uganda. The project required careful planning around how and where to place the system so that water could flow naturally down steep mountainous terrain, improving the community's access to clean drinking water through a gravity-fed distribution approach. Working within tight field constraints — limited materials, remote location, and challenging geography — I applied core engineering design principles and iterative problem-solving to arrive at a practical, sustainable solution that could be realistically constructed and maintained by the community with depleted resources.

02

STEM Accessible Garbage Can

Cornerstone of Engineering II · London, UK · Spring 2025

For my Cornerstone of Engineering II design project, my team and I designed and built a fully automated waste disposal system optimized for everyday use with a focus on enhanced accessibility for individuals with disabilities. The mechanical design was done entirely in SolidWorks, where I modeled custom components that were then 3D-printed and assembled together to form the final product — each piece had to fit precisely into the overall structure while remaining easy to manufacture and replace. The electronics side was powered by an Arduino microcontroller, with a circuit that integrated switches, motors, transmitters, and wiring all mounted to the base and lid. The project required constant iteration between the CAD model and the physical prototype, adjusting tolerances and rethinking component placement as we tested and refined each version.
